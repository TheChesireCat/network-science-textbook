# Chapter 4: Scale-Free Networks

## Section 4.1: Introduction

The World Wide Web is a network whose nodes are documents and links are URLs that allow us to navigate from one web document to another. With an estimated size of over one trillion documents ($N \sim 10^{12}$), the Web is the largest network humanity has ever built, exceeding even the human brain ($N \sim 10^{11}$ neurons).

The WWW played a crucial role in developing network theory by facilitating the discovery of fundamental network characteristics and serving as a standard testbed for network measures. A *crawler*—software that maps the Web's wiring diagram—starts from any web document, identifies its links, downloads the linked documents, and iteratively creates a local map of the Web.

The first systematic map of the WWW was generated by Hawoong Jeong at the University of Notre Dame, mapping the nd.edu domain with about 300,000 documents and 1.5 million links. While the Web's topology appeared random at first glance, closer inspection revealed puzzling differences: unlike random networks (which forbid hubs), the Web contained numerous small-degree nodes coexisting with a few exceptionally highly connected nodes called *hubs*.

In this chapter, we explore the *scale-free property*—a deeper organizing principle characterizing networks with many real examples. The degree distribution of real networks reveals this universal feature, which has profound implications for network robustness, spreading processes, and other properties.

## Section 4.2: Power Laws and Scale-Free Networks

The degree distribution of the WWW does not follow a Poisson distribution as expected for random networks. Instead, on a log-log scale, the data points form an approximate straight line, suggesting a *power law distribution*:

$$p_k \sim k^{-\gamma} \tag{4.1}$$

where $\gamma$ is the *degree exponent*. Taking logarithms:

$$\log p_k \sim -\gamma \log k \tag{4.2}$$

On a log-log plot, $\log p_k$ depends linearly on $\log k$, with slope equal to the degree exponent $\gamma$.

Since the WWW is directed, each document has an *out-degree* $k_{out}$ (links pointing from it) and an *in-degree* $k_{in}$ (links pointing to it). Both follow power laws:

$$p_{k_{in}} \sim k^{-\gamma_{in}} \tag{4.3}$$

$$p_{k_{out}} \sim k^{-\gamma_{out}} \tag{4.4}$$

For the WWW sample, $\gamma_{in} \approx 2.1$ and $\gamma_{out} \approx 2.45$.

**Definition:** A *scale-free network* is a network whose degree distribution follows a power law.

The power law persists for almost four orders of magnitude in the WWW, establishing it as scale-free. For the Web, this scale-free property applies to both in-degrees and out-degrees.

### Discrete Formalism

Since node degrees are positive integers, the discrete power-law distribution is:

$$p_k = Ck^{-\gamma} \tag{4.5}$$

The normalization condition is:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} p_k = 1 \tag{4.6}$$

This yields:

$$C = \frac{1}{\zeta(\gamma)} \tag{4.7}$$

where $\zeta(\gamma)$ is the Riemann zeta function. Thus:

$$p_k = \frac{k^{-\gamma}}{\zeta(\gamma)} \tag{4.8}$$

Note that (4.8) diverges at $k=0$; we can separately specify $p_0$ if needed.

### Continuum Formalism

For analytical calculations, we assume degrees can take any positive real value:

$$p(k) = Ck^{-\gamma} \tag{4.9}$$

Using the normalization condition:

$$\int_{k_{min}}^{\infty} p(k)dk = 1 \tag{4.10}$$

we obtain:

$$C = (\gamma - 1)k_{min}^{\gamma - 1} \tag{4.11}$$

Therefore:

$$p(k) = (\gamma - 1)k_{min}^{\gamma - 1} k^{-\gamma} \tag{4.12}$$

where $k_{min}$ is the smallest degree for which the power law holds. Only the integral of $p(k)$ has physical meaning:

$$\int_{k_1}^{k_2} p(k)dk \tag{4.13}$$

is the probability that a randomly chosen node has degree between $k_1$ and $k_2$.

### Box 4.1: The 80/20 Rule and the Top One Percent

Vilfredo Pareto, a 19th-century economist, observed that in Italy a few wealthy individuals earned most of the money while the majority earned small amounts. He connected this to a power-law income distribution—the first known report of a power law. This entered popular literature as the *80/20 rule*: roughly 80% of money is earned by 20% of the population.

The 80/20 rule appears across many domains. In networks: 80% of Web links point to 15% of webpages; 80% of citations go to 38% of scientists; 80% of Hollywood links involve 30% of actors. Most power-law distributions obey the 80/20 rule.

During the 2009 economic crisis, the "1% phenomenon" highlighted that in the US, 1% of the population earns a disproportionate 15% of total income—a consequence of power-law income distribution.

## Section 4.3: Hubs

The main difference between random and scale-free networks appears in the *tail* of the degree distribution (high-$k$ region):

- **Low $k$:** Power law is above the Poisson function, so scale-free networks have more small-degree nodes.
- **$k \approx \langle k \rangle$:** Poisson is above the power law, indicating excess of average-degree nodes in random networks.
- **High $k$:** Power law is again above Poisson, with dramatically higher probability of hubs.

To illustrate: For $k=100$, the Poisson probability is $p_{100} \sim 10^{-94}$ while the power-law probability is $p_{100} \sim 4 \times 10^{-4}$. If the WWW (with $\langle k \rangle = 4.6$ and $N \sim 10^{12}$) were random, we'd expect:

$$N_{k \geq 100} = 10^{12} \sum_{k=100}^{\infty} \frac{(4.6)^k}{k!}e^{-4.6} \simeq 10^{-82} \tag{4.14}$$

nodes with ≥100 links—effectively none. With the WWW's power law ($\gamma_{in} = 2.1$), we have $N_{k \geq 100} = 4 \times 10^9$—over four billion nodes.

### The Largest Hub

All real networks are finite. This prompts the question: How does network size affect hub size? We calculate the *maximum degree* $k_{max}$, called the *natural cutoff* of the degree distribution, representing the expected size of the largest hub.

For an exponential distribution $p(k) = Ce^{-\lambda k}$, assuming at most one node beyond $k_{max}$:

$$\int_{k_{max}}^{\infty} p(k)dk = \frac{1}{N} \tag{4.16}$$

This yields:

$$k_{max} = k_{min} + \frac{\ln N}{\lambda} \tag{4.17}$$

Since $\ln N$ grows slowly with system size, $k_{max}$ doesn't increase significantly. For a Poisson distribution, the dependence is even slower.

For a scale-free network:

$$k_{max} = k_{min} N^{\frac{1}{\gamma - 1}} \tag{4.18}$$

Larger networks have larger hubs! The polynomial dependence implies orders of magnitude differences between the smallest and largest nodes in large scale-free networks.

For the WWW sample ($N \sim 3 \times 10^5$, $k_{min}=1$, $\gamma=2.1$), equation (4.18) predicts $k_{max} \sim 95,000$. The actual maximum in-degree was 10,721, confirming scale-free predictions. **In random networks, hubs are effectively forbidden; in scale-free networks, they are naturally present.**

## Section 4.4: The Meaning of Scale-Free

The term "scale-free" comes from statistical physics and phase transitions. To understand it, we examine *moments* of the degree distribution:

$$\langle k^n \rangle = \sum_{k_{min}}^{\infty} k^n p_k \approx \int_{k_{min}}^{\infty} k^n p(k)dk \tag{4.19}$$

Lower moments have important interpretations:
- **$n=1$:** First moment is average degree $\langle k \rangle$
- **$n=2$:** Second moment $\langle k^2 \rangle$ determines variance $\sigma^2 = \langle k^2 \rangle - \langle k \rangle^2$
- **$n=3$:** Third moment determines skewness

For a scale-free network:

$$\langle k^n \rangle = \int_{k_{min}}^{k_{max}} k^n p(k)dk = C\frac{k_{max}^{n - \gamma + 1} - k_{min}^{n - \gamma + 1}}{n - \gamma + 1} \tag{4.20}$$

In the asymptotic limit $k_{max} \to \infty$ (very large networks):
- If $n - \gamma + 1 \leq 0$ (i.e., $n \leq \gamma - 1$), then $\langle k^n \rangle$ is finite
- If $n - \gamma + 1 > 0$ (i.e., $n > \gamma - 1$), then $\langle k^n \rangle$ diverges

For many scale-free networks with $2 < \gamma < 3$:
- First moment $\langle k \rangle$ is finite
- Second and higher moments diverge

This divergence explains the "scale-free" term. A randomly chosen node's degree in a normal distribution typically falls within $\langle k \rangle \pm \sigma_k$.

**Random Networks Have a Scale:**
For a Poisson distribution, $\sigma_k = \langle k \rangle^{1/2}$, which is smaller than $\langle k \rangle$. Nodes have comparable degrees with $\langle k \rangle$ serving as the "scale."

**Scale-free Networks Lack a Scale:**
For power-law distributions with $\gamma < 3$, the divergence of $\langle k^2 \rangle$ indicates that fluctuations around the average are arbitrary large. A randomly chosen node's degree is unpredictable—it could be tiny or arbitrarily large. Networks with $\gamma < 3$ do not have a meaningful internal scale and are "scale-free."

For the WWW ($\langle k \rangle = 4.60$, $\gamma \approx 2.1$), the second moment diverges, meaning the in-degree expectation is $k = 4.60 \pm \infty$. A random document could have degree 1-2 (74.02% have in-degree less than $\langle k \rangle$) or millions of links like Google or Facebook.

In the $N \to \infty$ limit, $\langle k^2 \rangle$ diverges only in that limit, but divergence is relevant for finite networks. Table 4.1 shows that for most real networks, $\sigma$ is significantly larger than $\langle k \rangle$. For example, in the WWW sample, the randomly chosen node degree is $k_{in} = 4.60 \pm 1546$—the average is not informative.

The scale-free name captures the *lack of an internal scale*, distinguishing scale-free networks from lattices (where all nodes have the same degree, $\sigma = 0$) and random networks (where $\sigma = \langle k \rangle^{1/2}$).

## Section 4.5: Universality

While "WWW" and "Internet" are often used interchangeably, they are different systems:
- **WWW:** Information network with documents as nodes and URLs as links
- **Internet:** Infrastructural network with routers as nodes and physical connections as links

Despite these differences, *both have power-law degree distributions*. The WWW's topology reveals the Internet's scale-free nature with highly connected routers holding together numerous small-degree routers.

Many networks of scientific, technological, and societal importance display the scale-free property:

The diversity of scale-free systems is remarkable: the WWW (decades old, man-made) and protein interaction networks (billions of years of evolution) both exhibit this property. This universality makes the scale-free property unavoidable across disciplines.

### Plotting the Degree Distribution

Degree distributions are plotted on double logarithmic (log-log) scales. When nodes have widely different degrees, linear plots cannot display all data. *Logarithmic binning* ensures each datapoint has sufficient observations.

### Measuring the Degree Exponent

A quick estimate comes from fitting a straight line to $p_k$ on a log-log plot, but this can have systematic biases. Statistical tools are discussed in Advanced Topics 4.C.

### The Shape of $p_k$ for Real Networks

Many real networks deviate from pure power laws, with low-degree saturation and high-degree cutoffs due to data incompleteness, collection biases, or underlying network processes.

**Summary:** Since the 1999 discovery of WWW scale-free nature, many networks—biological, social, linguistic, technological—have been found scale-free. Not all networks are scale-free, however; many important networks (power grid, materials networks) do not display this property.

## Section 4.6: Ultra-Small Property

Do hubs affect the small-world property? Airlines use hubs to decrease hops between airports, suggesting yes. Indeed, **distances in scale-free networks are smaller than in equivalent random networks**.

The average distance $\langle d \rangle$ dependence on system size $N$ and degree exponent $\gamma$ is:

$$\langle d \rangle \sim \begin{cases} 
\text{const.} & \gamma = 2 \\
\ln \ln N & 2 < \gamma < 3 \\
\frac{\ln N}{\ln \ln N} & \gamma = 3 \\
\ln N & \gamma > 3
\end{cases} \tag{4.22}$$

### Anomalous Regime ($\gamma = 2$)

For $\gamma = 2$, $k_{max} \sim N$, forcing a *hub and spoke* configuration where all nodes are close to each other through a central hub. Average path length is independent of $N$.

### Ultra-Small World ($2 < \gamma < 3$)

Here $\langle d \rangle \sim \ln \ln N$, significantly slower than the $\ln N$ for random networks. We call these *ultra-small* networks. Hubs radically reduce path length by linking many small-degree nodes.

For the world's social network ($N \sim 7 \times 10^9$): random network gives $\ln N = 22.66$; scale-free gives $\ln \ln N = 3.12$—hubs dramatically shrink distances.

### Critical Point ($\gamma = 3$)

At this critical point, $\langle k^2 \rangle$ no longer diverges. The $\ln N$ dependence of random networks returns, but with a double logarithmic correction $\ln \ln N$, shrinking distances compared to random networks.

### Small World ($\gamma > 3$)

Here $\langle k^2 \rangle$ is finite and $\langle d \rangle$ follows the small-world formula for random networks. Hubs continue to exist but aren't large/numerous enough to significantly impact distances.

More pronounced hubs more effectively shrink distances. For small $N$, distances in the four regimes are comparable; for large $N$, remarkable differences appear.

Frigyes Karinthy's 1929 short story noted it's "always easier to find someone who knows a famous or popular figure than some run-of-the-mill person." In scale-free networks, we are typically closer to hubs than to less-connected nodes.

Many shortest paths go through hubs. However, experimental evidence shows individuals in chain experiments were *less* likely to send messages to hubs than those in incomplete chains—we perceive hubs as busy and avoid contacting them unnecessarily.

## Section 4.7: The Role of the Degree Exponent

Table 4.1 shows $\gamma$ varies across systems, and most have $\gamma > 2$. We explore how scale-free properties change with $\gamma$.

### Anomalous Regime ($\gamma \leq 2$)

For $\gamma \leq 2$, the exponent $1/(\gamma - 1) > 1$ in (4.18), so the largest hub degree grows faster than network size. Eventually, the largest hub would need more links than exist nodes, requiring multi-links or self-loops. For $\gamma \leq 2$, $\langle k \rangle$ diverges.

Large-scale networks with $\gamma \leq 2$ lacking multi-links cannot exist; degree distributions with $\gamma \leq 2$ are not *graphical*.

### Scale-Free Regime ($2 < \gamma < 3$)

Here $\langle k \rangle$ is finite but $\langle k^2 \rangle$ diverges. These networks are ultra-small. From (4.18), $k_{max}$ grows with exponent less than 1, so the largest hub's market share ($k_{max}/N$) decreases with network size. Many interesting scale-free properties—from robustness to anomalous spreading—occur in this regime.

### Random Network Regime ($\gamma > 3$)

Both first and second moments are finite. Scale-free network properties are difficult to distinguish from random networks with similar size. Distances converge to the small-world formula; degree distribution decays fast enough to make hubs small and rare.

Networks with large $\gamma$ are hard to distinguish from random networks. To document power-law nature, we need 2-3 orders of magnitude scaling ($k_{max}$ should be $10^2$–$10^3$ times $k_{min}$). The network size needed is:

$$N = \left(\frac{k_{max}}{k_{min}}\right)^{\gamma - 1} \tag{4.23}$$

For $\gamma = 5$ with two orders of magnitude scaling (e.g., $k_{min} \sim 1$, $k_{max} \sim 100$), we need $N > 10^8$. Few network maps exist at this size, so many networks may have large $\gamma$ with insufficient data to prove scale-free nature.

**Summary:** Scale-free network behavior depends on $\gamma$. The theoretically most interesting regime is $2 < \gamma < 3$ (where $\langle k^2 \rangle$ diverges), and many practical networks fall here, including the WWW and protein interaction networks.

### Box 4.6: Why Scale-Free Networks With $\gamma \leq 2$ Do Not Exist

A degree sequence that can form a simple graph (without multi-links or self-loops) is called *graphical*. Not all degree sequences are graphical; for example, if the total number of stubs is odd, one remains unmatched.

The Erdős-Gallai algorithm tests graphicality. When applied to scale-free networks, the fraction of graphical degree sequences drops to zero for $\gamma \leq 2$. For these networks, the largest hub degree grows faster than $N$; without allowing self-loops and multi-links, the largest hub runs out of nodes to connect to.

## Section 4.8: Generating Networks with Arbitrary Degree Distribution

Erdős-Rényi networks have Poisson degree distributions. Real networks significantly deviate from Poisson form, raising the question: How do we generate networks with arbitrary $p_k$? Three algorithms address this:

### Configuration Model

The configuration model builds networks with predefined degree sequences. The algorithm:
1. Assign degrees to nodes as "stubs" (half-links). The degree sequence is either analytically generated from $p_k$ or extracted from a real network. Start with an even number of stubs.
2. Randomly select stub pairs and connect them. Repeat until all stubs pair up.

Different networks result from different stub orderings. Some include cycles, self-loops, or multi-links. The expected number of self-loops and multi-links approaches zero as $N \to \infty$.

The probability of a link between nodes of degrees $k_i$ and $k_j$ is:

$$p_{ij} = \frac{k_i k_j}{2L - 1} \tag{4.24}$$

The configuration model frequently generates self-loops and multi-links, but these remain negligible. The model's random character helps analytically calculate network measures.

### Degree-Preserving Randomization

To test if a network property results from its degree distribution or represents additional structure, we generate networks with the same $p_k$ but randomized wiring. *Degree-preserving randomization* does this by randomly selecting two links and swapping their endpoints (if no multi-links result). Each node retains its degree, but the network is rewired.

This differs from *full randomization*, which randomly rewires without preserving degrees, creating an Erdős-Rényi network with Poisson degree distribution.

![Degree Preserving Randomization](https://networksciencebook.com/images/ch-04/figure-4-17.jpg)

*Image 4.17: Two randomization algorithms with different outcomes. (a) Full randomization generates random networks with the same $N$ and $L$, changing node degrees. (b) Degree-preserving randomization maintains each node's degree but randomizes the wiring. Bottom panels: Starting from a scale-free network (middle), full randomization eliminates hubs (left), while degree-preserving randomization preserves hubs (right).*

### Hidden Parameter Model

The *hidden parameter model* generates networks with predefined $p_k$ without multi-links or self-loops. Assign each node $i$ a hidden parameter $\eta_i$ from distribution $\rho(\eta)$. Connect each node pair with probability:

$$p(\eta_i, \eta_j) = \frac{\eta_i \eta_j}{\langle \eta \rangle N}$$

Two approaches generate hidden parameters:

1. **Random selection:** $\eta_i$ drawn from $\rho(\eta)$. The degree distribution is:
$$p_k = \int \frac{e^{-\eta} \eta^k}{k!} \rho(\eta) d\eta \tag{4.26}$$

2. **Deterministic sequence:** $\eta_i = c/i^\alpha$ for $i = 1, ..., N$. The degree distribution is:
$$p_k = \frac{1}{N}\sum_j \frac{e^{-\eta_j} \eta_j^k}{k!} \tag{4.27}$$

With $\eta_j = c/i^\alpha$:

$$p_k \sim k^{-(1 + 1/\alpha)} \tag{4.29}$$

So by choosing appropriate $\alpha$, we tune $\gamma = 1 + 1/\alpha$. We can also use $\langle \eta \rangle$ to tune $\langle k \rangle$ since (4.26) and (4.27) imply $\langle k \rangle = \langle \eta \rangle$.

![Hidden Parameter Model](https://networksciencebook.com/images/ch-04/figure-4-18.jpg)

The choice depends on whether you start from a degree sequence $\{k_i\}$ or distribution $p_k$, and whether self-loops and multi-links are tolerable.

### Box 4.7: Generating a Degree Sequence with Power-Law Distribution

The *degree sequence* is the list of node degrees. To generate a sequence from $p_k$, calculate:

$$D(k) = \sum_{k' \geq k} p_{k'} \tag{4.25}$$

$D(k)$ is between 0 and 1 with step size $p_k$ at each $k$. To generate $N$ degrees following $p_k$:
1. Generate $N$ random numbers $r_i$ uniformly from (0,1)
2. For each $r_i$, use the $D(k)$ plot to assign degree $k_i = D^{-1}(r_i)$

The obtained sequence follows the desired $p_k$.

### Box 4.8: Testing the Small-World Property

When distances in real networks are compared to the small-world formula (3.19), we must remember it was derived for random networks. Degree-preserving randomization helps test if observed distances are smaller than expected from degree distribution alone:

1. **Original network:** Measure distance distribution $p_d$, obtaining $\langle d \rangle = 5.61$ for protein interactions.

2. **Full randomization:** Generate random network with same $N$ and $L$. We get $\langle d \rangle = 7.13$, much larger. It seems an organizing principle shortens distances—but this overlooks that full randomization changed the degree distribution.

3. **Degree-preserving randomization:** Maintain original degree distribution. We get $p_d$ comparable to original, $\langle d \rangle = 5.08 \pm 1.34$, showing distances result primarily from the degree distribution.

**Key finding:** Random networks overestimate distances by missing hubs. Degree-preserving randomization retains hubs, matching the original network's distances.

- They don't explain the origin of observed $p_k$
- Important characteristics like clustering and degree correlations are lost during randomization

However, networks generated by these algorithms are like photographs of paintings: at first glance they appear the same, but many details are lost upon closer inspection.

## Section 4.9: Summary

The scale-free property's importance in network science stems from two factors:

1. **Universality:** Many networks of scientific and practical interest—from the WWW to subcellular networks—are scale-free
2. **Impact:** Hubs fundamentally change system behavior, as the ultra-small property first hints. Many more examples appear in coming chapters.

We must remember that pure power-law form (4.1) is rare in real systems. Numerous processes affect each network's topology and degree distribution shape. Rather than focusing narrowly on power-law fit quality, we distinguish two network classes:

### Exponentially Bounded Networks

Networks with degree distributions decreasing exponentially or faster have $\langle k^2 \rangle < \langle k \rangle$, lacking significant degree variations. Examples include Poisson, Gaussian, and simple exponential distributions. Erdős-Rényi and Watts-Strogatz networks belong here. Real examples: highway networks, power grids. Exponentially bounded networks lack outliers; most nodes have comparable degrees.

### Fat-Tailed Networks

Networks with power-law tails have $\langle k^2 \rangle \gg \langle k \rangle$, resulting in large degree variations. Scale-free networks are the best examples. These networks expect outliers—exceptionally high-degree nodes. Real examples: WWW, Internet, protein interactions, most social and online networks.

For network understanding, it often suffices to decide if a degree distribution is exponentially bounded or fat-tailed. If exponentially bounded, random networks offer reasonable starting points. If fat-tailed, scale-free networks are better approximations. The key signature is the magnitude of $\langle k^2 \rangle$: if large, systems behave like scale-free networks; if comparable to $\langle k \rangle(\langle t \rangle + 1)$, they resemble random networks.

**In scale-free networks, a few highly connected hubs coexist with many small nodes. These hubs play important roles in system behavior.** This chapter explored basic scale-free characteristics. The remaining question is: Why are so many real networks scale-free? The next chapter provides the answer.

### Box 4.9: At a Glance: Scale-Free Networks

**Degree Distribution:**

Discrete form: $p_k = \frac{k^{-\gamma}}{\zeta(\gamma)}$

Continuous form: $p(k) = (\gamma - 1)k_{min}^{\gamma - 1} k^{-\gamma}$

**Size of the Largest Hub:**

$$k_{max} = k_{min} N^{\frac{1}{\gamma - 1}}$$

**Moments of $p_k$ for $N \to \infty$:**

$2 < \gamma \leq 3$: $\langle k \rangle$ finite, $\langle k^2 \rangle$ diverges
$\gamma > 3$: Both $\langle k \rangle$ and $\langle k^2 \rangle$ finite

**Distance:**

$$\langle d \rangle \sim \begin{cases}
\text{const.} & \gamma = 2 \\
\ln \ln N & 2 < \gamma < 3 \\
\frac{\ln N}{\ln \ln N} & \gamma = 3 \\
\ln N & \gamma > 3
\end{cases}$$

## Section 4.11: Advanced Topic 4.A — Power Laws

Power laws have convoluted history in natural and social sciences, called fat-tailed, heavy-tailed, long-tailed, Pareto, or Bradford distributions. They have relatives like log-normal, Weibull, or Lévy distributions.

### Exponentially Bounded Distributions

Many quantities follow bounded distributions where $p_x$ decays exponentially or faster for high $x$. The largest expected $x$ after $N$ draws grows as $x_{max} \sim \log N$ or slower. Outliers are rare—effectively forbidden. These *thin-tailed* distributions include exponential and Poisson (which describe random network degrees) and Gaussian.

### Fat-Tailed Distributions

Terms fat-tailed, heavy-tailed, or long-tailed refer to $p_x$ with decay slower than exponential. Events with very large $x$ ("outliers" or "rare events") occur frequently. Power-law distribution (4.1) is the best example. Largest event size after $N$ trials scales as $x_{max} \sim N^\zeta$ where $\zeta$ depends on $\gamma$, growing faster than for bounded distributions. Rare events occur with noticeable frequency, dominating system properties.

Power-law relevance to networks:
- Many network quantities follow power laws (degrees, link weights, betweenness)
- Power-law form is analytically predicted by network models

### Crossover Distributions (Log-Normal, Stretched Exponential)

When empirical distributions lie between power law and exponential, *crossover distributions* fit the data.

**Power law with exponential cut-off:**

$$p(x) = Cx^{-\gamma} e^{-\lambda x} \tag{4.30}$$

$$C = \frac{\lambda^{1-\gamma}}{\Gamma(1-\gamma, \lambda x_{min})} \tag{4.31}$$

Taking logarithm:

$$\ln p(x) = \ln C - \gamma \ln x - \lambda x \tag{4.32}$$

For $x \ll 1/\lambda$, the power-law term dominates ($\sim k^{-\gamma}$). For $x \gg 1/\lambda$, the exponential term dominates, creating an exponential cutoff.

**Stretched exponential (Weibull distribution):**

$$P'(x) = Cx^{\beta - 1} e^{-(\lambda x)^\beta} \tag{4.33}$$

$$C = \beta \lambda^\beta \tag{4.34}$$

Here $\beta$ is the *stretching exponent*:
- $\beta = 1$: Simple exponential
- $0 < \beta < 1$: "Stretched" exponential, difficult to distinguish from pure power law; closer to $k^{-1}$ as $\beta$ decreases
- $\beta > 1$: "Compressed" exponential with narrow $x$ range
- $\beta = 2$: Rayleigh distribution

Several network models predict stretched exponential degree distributions.

**Log-normal distribution:**

Emerges if $\ln x$ follows a normal distribution. Appears when a variable is the product of many independent positive random numbers (e.g., compound investment returns). The probability density is:

$$p(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi} \sigma x} \exp\left[-\frac{(\ln x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right] \tag{4.35}$$

The parameter

$$\sigma^2 = \langle (\ln x)^2 \rangle - \langle \ln x \rangle^2 \tag{4.36}$$

captures typical order-of-magnitude variation in $x$. For large $\sigma$, log-normal resembles power law across several orders of magnitude. Like power laws, $\langle x^2 \rangle$ grows exponentially with $\sigma$, becoming very large.

**Summary:** Fat-tailed distributions debate—whether power law, stretched exponential, or log-normal—is ongoing when empirical data is insufficient. This debate is resolved by accurate mechanistic models analytically predicting expected distributions. Network models predict Poisson, exponential, stretched exponential, and power-law distributions. Other distributions, while sometimes used to fit network degrees, lack theoretical basis for network relevance.

## Section 4.12: Advanced Topic 4.B — Plotting Power Laws

Plotting degree distributions is integral to network analysis. The process starts with obtaining $N_k$ (number of nodes with degree $k$) and calculating $p_k = N_k/N$. The question is how to best plot $p_k$ to extract its properties.

### Use a Log-Log Plot

Linear plots compress small-degree nodes and make large-$k$ values appear zero. Log-log plots avoid these problems, showing all scales clearly. Points with $p_k = 0$ or $k = 0$ don't appear (since $\log 0 = -\infty$).

### Avoid Linear Binning

Linear binning (plotting $p_k = N_k/N$ directly) creates a horizontal plateau at large $k$: with one node per high-degree value, we have either $N_k = 0$ (not shown on log-log) or $N_k = 1$ (giving $p_k = 1/N$ for all hubs, creating a plateau). This plateau biases degree exponent estimation by affecting the high-$k$ regime that determines $\gamma$.

### Use Logarithmic Binning

Log-binning increases bin sizes with degree, ensuring comparable node numbers per bin. For example, using powers of 2: bin 0 has size 1 ($k=1$), bin 1 has size 2 ($k=2,3$), bin 2 has size 4 ($k=4,5,6,7$), etc. Generally, bin $n$ has size $2^n$ and the degree distribution is $p_{\langle k_n \rangle} = N_n/b_n$, where $N_n$ is nodes in bin $n$, $b_n$ is bin size, and $\langle k_n \rangle$ is average degree in the bin.

Log-binned $p_k$ shows scaling extending into the high-$k$ plateau, extracting useful information from rare high-degree nodes.

### Use Cumulative Distribution

The complementary cumulative distribution

$$\tilde{p}_k = \sum_{q=k+1}^{\infty} p_q$$

enhances the high-degree region's statistical significance. For power-law $p_k \sim k^{-\gamma}$, the cumulative scales as $\tilde{p}_k \sim k^{-\gamma + 1}$, eliminating the linear binning plateau and extending the scaling region.

![Plotting a Degree Distribution](https://networksciencebook.com/images/ch-04/figure-4-22.jpg)

*Image 4.22: A degree distribution $p_k \sim (k + k_0)^{-\gamma}$ with $k_0 = 10$ and $\gamma = 2.5$, plotted four ways: (a) Linear scale, linear binning—distribution invisible. (b) Log-log, linear binning—tail visible but with plateau. (c) Log-log, log-binning—plateau disappears, scaling extends to high-$k$. (d) Log-log, cumulative—clean scaling region.*

### Box 4.10: Degree Distribution of Real Networks

Real systems rarely show pure power laws. Common deviations include:

**Low-degree saturation:** Flattened $p_k$ for $k < k_{sat}$ indicates fewer small-degree nodes than expected from pure power law. Origins explained in Chapter 6.

**High-degree cutoff:** Rapid drop in $p_k$ for $k > k_{cut}$ limits the largest hub below (4.18) predictions. Emerges from inherent link limitations (e.g., social networks limit meaningful relationships).

Given widespread cutoffs, distributions are fitted to:

$$p_k = a(k + k_{sat})^{-\gamma} \exp\left(-\frac{k}{k_{cut}}\right) \tag{4.39}$$

To extract full scaling, plot:

$$\tilde{p}_k = p_k \exp\left(\frac{k}{k_{cut}}\right) \tag{4.40}$$

versus $\tilde{k} = k + k_{sat}$. According to (4.40), $\tilde{p} \sim \tilde{k}^{-\gamma}$, correcting for both cutoffs.

Cutoff presence does not mean the network isn't scale-free. Scale-free network properties are insensitive to low-degree saturation; high-degree cutoff limits $\langle k^2 \rangle$ divergence, indicating additional phenomena to understand.

![Rescaling the Degree Distribution](https://networksciencebook.com/images/ch-04/figure-4-23.jpg)

*Image 4.23: (a) Real networks frequently show low-degree saturation and high-degree cutoff. (b) Plotting the rescaled $\tilde{p}_k$ versus $(\tilde{k})$ reveals power-law behavior across all degrees.*

## Section 4.13: Advanced Topic 4.C — Estimating the Degree Exponent

Since scale-free network properties depend on $\gamma$, we need tools to determine it. Fitting a straight line to log-log $p_k$ can have systematic biases. Here we discuss statistical methods, illustrated with a citation network (384,362 papers with 2,353,984 citations from 1890–2009).

### Fitting Procedure

1. Choose $K_{min}$ between $k_{min}$ and $k_{max}$. Estimate the degree exponent:

$$\gamma = 1 + N\left[\sum_{i=1}^N \ln\frac{k_i}{K_{min} - 1/2}\right]^{-1} \tag{4.41}$$

2. Assume the distribution (4.42) and calculate the cumulative distribution (4.43):

$$p_k = \frac{1}{\zeta(\gamma, K_{min})} k^{-\gamma} \tag{4.42}$$

$$P_k = 1 - \frac{\zeta(\gamma, k)}{\zeta(\gamma, K_{min})} \tag{4.43}$$

3. Use the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to find the maximum distance between the empirical CDF $S(k)$ and the fitted model:

$$D = \max_{k \geq K_{min}} |S(k) - P_k| \tag{4.44}$$

4. Repeat steps 1–3, scanning the full $K_{min}$ range. Find the $K_{min}$ value minimizing $D$. The corresponding $\gamma$ represents the optimal fit.

The standard error is:

$$\sigma_\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N\left[\frac{\zeta''(\gamma, K_{min})}{\zeta(\gamma, K_{min})} - \left(\frac{\zeta'(\gamma, K_{min})}{\zeta(\gamma, K_{min})}\right)^2\right]}} \tag{4.45}$$

For the citation network: $K_{min} = 49$ minimizes $D$, giving $\gamma = 2.79$ with standard error $\sigma_\gamma = 0.003$, so $\gamma = 2.79(3)$. Datasets smaller than $N = 50$ require caution.

### Goodness-of-Fit

Obtaining a best-fit $(\gamma, K_{min})$ pair doesn't mean the power law is good model. A goodness-of-fit test generates a $p$-value quantifying power-law hypothesis plausibility:

1. Calculate the KS distance $D^{real}$ between real data and best fit. For citations: $D^{real} = 0.01158$.

2. Generate a degree sequence of $N$ numbers from (4.42) and calculate the corresponding $D^{synthetic}$.

3. Repeat step 2 many times ($M \gg 1$) to obtain $p(D^{synthetic})$ distribution. If $D^{real}$ is within this distribution, the power law is reasonable; if outside, another function better describes $p_k$.

Assign a $p$-number:

$$p = \int_{D^{real}}^{\infty} P(D^{synthetic}) dD^{synthetic} \tag{4.46}$$

Close to 1 means power law is plausible; very small $p$ means it's not. Typically, $p > 1\%$ is accepted as statistically significant.

For the citation network, $p < 10^{-4}$, indicating a pure power law is unsuitable, surprising given power law documentation since the 1960s. This failure shows the limitation of blind power-law fitting without understanding the underlying distribution.

### Fitting Real Distributions

To correct this, note that fitting model (4.42) discards all $k < K_{min}$ data. Yet, citation networks (fat-tailed) have statistically useful information in the low-$k$ regime. Using a fitting model that treats the whole distribution:

$$p_k = \frac{1}{\sum_{k'=1} (k' + k_{sat})^{-\gamma} e^{-k'/k_{cut}}} (k + k_{sat})^{-\gamma} e^{-k/k_{cut}} \tag{4.47}$$

with associated CDF (4.48), we find fitting parameters $k_{sat}, k_{cut}, \gamma$ via:

1. Pick $k_{sat}$ and $k_{cut}$ values. Estimate $\gamma$ maximizing the log-likelihood:

$$\log L(\gamma|k_{sat}, k_{cut}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log p(k_i|\gamma, k_{sat}, k_{cut})$$

2. Calculate the KS distance $D$ between the data CDF and fitted model (4.47).

3. Scan through $k_{sat}$ and $k_{cut}$ ranges to find values minimizing $D$.

For the citation network: optimal fit is $k_{sat} = 12$, $k_{cut} = 5691$, giving $\gamma = 3.028$ with $p = 69\%$, confirming the power law is suitable.

*Image 4.24: (a) Citation network degree distribution with best-fit power law. (b) KS test values vs. $K_{min}$, showing minimum at $K_{min} = 49$. (c) $p(D^{synthetic})$ for 10,000 synthetic datasets; grey line shows $D^{real}$ from citations.*

### Systematic Fitting Issues

Determining the degree exponent has well-known limitations:

1. **Pure power law is idealized.** Real networks have saturation and cutoffs. If $p_k$ doesn't follow pure power law, fitting procedures fail to detect significance. This usually means we haven't understood the true $p_k$ form, not that the network isn't scale-free.

2. **KS test limitations:** KS measures maximum distance between fitted model and dataset. If almost all points follow perfect power law but a single outlier deviates, statistical significance is lost—even if that outlier is unimportant.

3. **Outlier example:** The actor network's degree distribution follows power law but has a prominent outlier at $k = 1,287$ due to the 1956 film "Around the World in Eighty Days," which listed all extras in the cast (normally uncredited). The second-largest movie had only 340 actors. This outlier causes the power-law fit to fail KS criteria, yet doesn't alter the overall power-law nature.

4. **Scaling regime problems:** The fitting methodology often predicts small scaling regimes, requiring removal of huge data fractions (often 99% or more) for statistical significance. The resulting fit, when plotted beside original data, can appear ridiculous despite predicted statistical significance.
